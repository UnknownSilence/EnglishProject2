Chickamauga | The Boy

The Torso

Since the child is so young, he really only has innocent instincts when he enters the woods. For the endurance of his life, he has lived a very sheltered life, being protected from the realities of life by his mother and the house/field servants. When the boy encounters the crawling men, the audience sees his true instincts, as he “approached one of these crawling figures from behind and with an agile movement mounted it astride” (Bierce 2). The boy is too young and too inexperienced to really understand the severity of this situation, and although the audience views this as childish, this is the only reality he knows of, as “he had seen his father’s negroes creep upon their hands and knees for his amusement-- had ridden them so, “making believe” they were his horses” (Bierce 2). The character is too young to be self-conscious of his actions or thoughts, or hide things from others in society and his life. However, I believe that the little boy is pained by the destruction of his childhood/innocence. At the end of the story, the little boy sees his home in flames, along with his dead mother in the yard, causing him to throw his sword, once considered a symbol of his childhood as he played a war game, into the fire as “a surrender to the superior forces of nature,” which could be considered to be the realization of the real world (Bierce 3). Although he faces the dead bodies and wounded soldiers with “childish curiosity,” he views simple creatures such as a small white rabbit as a threat and runs off in fear (Bierce 2). His oblivion from the real world causes him to fear the harmless and his belief that the soldiers are acting out scenes from the war as “a merry spectacle” (Bierce 2).
